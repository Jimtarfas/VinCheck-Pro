## Body (Portable Text — paste as formatted content)

**Quick answer:** On any vehicle built since 1981, a chassis number and a VIN are the
**same 17-character code**. "Chassis number" is the term used in the UK, Europe,
Australia, India, the Middle East, and Africa; "VIN" (Vehicle Identification Number)
is the North American name and the technical/legal name everywhere. Same code, two names.

### Why the two names exist

When the international VIN standard (ISO 3779) was finalised in 1981, every market
adopted the same 17-character format — but kept its own everyday word for it. Your
registration document reflects local habit: a UK **V5C** says "chassis number," a US
**title** says "VIN," an Indian **RC** lists a "chassis number" alongside a separate
engine number. The code underneath is identical.

> If it's exactly 17 characters and contains no letters I, O, or Q, it's a modern
> chassis number — and it's a VIN.

### How to find your chassis number

It's deliberately repeated in several places so the car and its paperwork can be matched:

- **Registration document** — V5C (UK), registration certificate (EU), RC (India), title (US/Canada)
- **Through the windscreen** — lower driver's-side corner on most modern cars
- **Engine bay** — on the manufacturer's plate
- **Chassis rail or firewall** — stamped into the metal
- **Driver-side door jamb** — on the service sticker

Every copy should match. A mismatch is one of the clearest signs of a cloned or
stolen vehicle.

### What the 17 characters tell you

A chassis number is a structured code, not a random serial:

- **1–3 (WMI):** manufacturer + country of origin
- **4–8 (VDS):** model, body style, engine, restraint system — the "car type"
- **9:** a check digit that validates the whole number
- **10:** the model year
- **11:** the assembly plant
- **12–17:** the unique serial number

That's why entering it into a decoder instantly returns the make, model, year, and
engine — the tool is reading these segments. (Full breakdown in our
[guide to reading a VIN](/guides/how-to-read-a-vin).)

### How to look up a car by chassis number

1. Locate the number (see above) and copy all 17 characters exactly — remember I, O, Q are never used.
2. Enter it into our free [Chassis Number Lookup](/chassis-number-lookup).
3. Read the decoded car type and confirm it matches the listing or document.
4. For a purchase, extend it into a full [VIN history check](/vin-check) for title, mileage, accident, and theft records.

### Chassis number vs engine number vs frame number

These get confused constantly:

- **Chassis number (VIN)** — identifies the *whole vehicle*; used for registration, history, and recalls. **This is the one to use.**
- **Engine number** — identifies the *engine block only*; can change if the engine is replaced.
- **Frame number** — another name for the chassis number on body-on-frame vehicles and motorcycles.

### When the chassis number is shorter than 17 characters

Pre-1981 cars, some classics, kit cars, and certain domestic-market vehicles
(e.g. Japanese JDM cars) use a shorter, non-standard chassis number. Those can't be
auto-decoded and need manufacturer records or a marque club. For a Japanese import,
pair the number with our [JDM import check](/jdm-import-check).

### The bottom line

Don't let the wording throw you. Whether the seller, the logbook, or the dealer calls
it a chassis number, a frame number, or a VIN, it's the same 17 characters — and you
can [decode it free in seconds](/chassis-number-lookup) to confirm exactly what the
car is before you commit.
